Macbeth — Quotes
Context: Written for King James I, who wrote a book on witchcraft. Jacobeans believed in the Divine Right of Kings — killing a king was a sin against God, not just murder. Keep this in every answer.
👻 Supernatural
Quote 1
"Fair is foul, and foul is fair"
Opens the play by inverting moral order. Good and evil are reversed — nothing can be trusted. Sets up the entire theme of deception. Shakespeare signals from line one that reality in this world is corrupted.
Quote 2
"Is this a dagger which I see before me?"
Macbeth hallucinates before killing Duncan. The supernatural is now inside his mind — guilt and ambition are indistinguishable from reality. He cannot trust his own senses.
Quote 3
"Something wicked this way comes"
The witches say this about Macbeth in Act 4. He has become so corrupted that even they recognise him as wicked. The supernatural created a monster from a hero.
Quote 4
"None of woman born shall harm Macbeth"
False security. The witches mislead with technically true language — Macduff was born by caesarean. Macbeth hears what he wants to hear. Language itself becomes a fatal trap.
👑 Power
Quote 1
"Vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself"
Macbeth's own soliloquy — he names his fatal flaw before he acts. No moral case for the murder, just pure ambition. This self-awareness makes him a tragic hero: he sees it and does it anyway.
Quote 2
"Stars, hide your fires; let not light see my black and deep desires"
He hides his ambition from God and the world. Power-hunger forces him into secrecy, isolation and eventually madness. He cannot be honest even with himself.
Quote 3
"To be thus is nothing; but to be safely thus"
Once king, Macbeth is still paranoid. Power brings no peace — just more fear. Shakespeare's warning: power gained through evil produces only anxiety, never satisfaction.
Quote 4
"Upon my head they placed a fruitless crown"
No heir. His power will die with him. All the murder achieved nothing lasting — a hollow, sterile throne. Power gained wrongly produces nothing of value.
💅 Lady Macbeth
Quote 1
"Unsex me here… fill me from the crown to the toe top-full of direst cruelty"
She invokes evil spirits to strip her femininity so she can act without remorse. She knows she must be transformed to commit this act — a Jacobean woman cannot do this alone.
Quote 2
"Look like the innocent flower, but be the serpent under't"
She coaches Macbeth in deception. She's the strategist, more cold-blooded than him at this point. Links back to "fair is foul" — appearances are weaponised.
Quote 3
"When you durst do it, then you were a man"
She attacks his masculinity to manipulate him into the murder. Psychological control — she exploits his insecurity about proving himself. This is her real power over him.
Quote 4
"Out, damned spot! Out, I say!"
Sleepwalking scene. Imagines blood she cannot wash away. The guilt she suppressed so effectively has broken her mind. She is destroyed by the very thing she helped create.

Higher mark points — say these in answers

Macbeth is a tragic hero — heroic at start, corrupted by a fatal flaw (ambition), falls from grace, dies
Jacobean audience would be horrified — regicide is a sin against God (Divine Right of Kings)
The witches don't force him — they trigger what's already inside him. He has free will.
Lady Macbeth subverts Jacobean norms (women = passive) but ultimately confirms them when she collapses
Macbeth — Key Moments
Remember: You don't need every scene. Know these 6 moments cold. For each one, know what it shows about the themes — Supernatural, Power, or Lady Macbeth.
1
The Witches Meet Macbeth (Act 1, Scene 3)
The inciting incident. The witches predict Macbeth will be Thane of Cawdor, then king. The first prophecy immediately comes true — this is what makes him believe them. He didn't plan to kill Duncan until this moment. Links to: Supernatural (the witches trigger his ambition) and Power (the first seed of dangerous ambition is planted).
"All hail, Macbeth, thou shalt be king hereafter!"
2
Lady Macbeth Plans the Murder (Act 1, Scene 5–7)
She reads Macbeth's letter, immediately decides Duncan must die, and invokes evil spirits to strengthen herself. When Macbeth hesitates, she manipulates him through his masculinity. This is her at her most powerful — calculating, cold, in total control. Links to: Lady Macbeth (her ambition and manipulation) and Power (who actually holds the power in this relationship).
"Unsex me here" / "When you durst do it, then you were a man"
3
Duncan is Murdered (Act 2, Scene 1–2)
Macbeth hallucinates a dagger before the murder. Afterwards he is shattered — cannot say 'Amen', hears voices, refuses to return the daggers. Lady Macbeth has to take charge. This is the point of no return. Links to: Supernatural (hallucinations, voices — the supernatural is now in his mind) and Power (Macbeth gains the crown but immediately loses his peace).
"Is this a dagger which I see before me?" / "Macbeth does murder sleep"
4
Banquo's Ghost (Act 3, Scene 4)
At a royal banquet, Macbeth sees Banquo's ghost and breaks down publicly. He alone can see it — everyone else thinks he's having a breakdown. Lady Macbeth tries to cover for him but fails. His mental state is now public. Links to: Supernatural (hallucination reveals guilt) and Power (his kingship starts to visibly collapse).
"Thou canst not say I did it; never shake thy gory locks at me"
5
Lady Macbeth's Sleepwalking (Act 5, Scene 1)
Lady Macbeth wanders at night, obsessively trying to wash imaginary blood from her hands. She re-enacts the murder out of order in fragments. All the guilt she suppressed has broken through in sleep when her conscious mind can't control it. She dies shortly after. Links to: Lady Macbeth (her arc from power to collapse) and Supernatural (guilt manifests physically, like blood that won't wash off).
"Out, damned spot! Out, I say!"
6
Macbeth's Death (Act 5, Scene 8)
Macduff reveals he was 'from his mother's womb untimely ripped' — the loophole in the prophecy. Macbeth fights and dies. Malcolm is crowned, order restored. The tragic arc is complete: the heroic Macbeth of Act 1 is now 'this dead butcher'. Links to: Power (the wrong kind of power always destroys itself) and Supernatural (the witches' prophecy comes true — but not in the way Macbeth expected).
"Yet I will try the last" / "this dead butcher and his fiend-like queen"

The arc in one sentence per theme

Supernatural: Triggers ambition → corrupts reality → creates monsters → fulfils fate ironically
Power: Gained wrongly → produces only paranoia → becomes tyranny → collapses
Lady Macbeth: Starts as the stronger partner → manipulates successfully → guilt destroys her first
A Christmas Carol — Quotes
Context: Published 1843. Victorian England — child labour, brutal workhouses, massive wealth gap. Dickens had worked in a factory as a child. He chose a story over a pamphlet because emotion changes minds that facts can't reach.
👻 Supernatural
Quote 1 — Marley
"I wear the chain I forged in life"
Marley's chain — made of cash-boxes, ledgers, padlocks — is his sins made physical. Allegory: greed imprisons you even in death. A direct warning to Scrooge of his own fate if he doesn't change.
Quote 2 — Ghost of Christmas Past
"like a child: yet not so like a child as like an old man"
Contradictory appearance mirrors the nature of memory — simultaneously young and old. Represents Scrooge's past: innocent at first, then corrupted by the pursuit of wealth.
Quote 3 — Ignorance & Want
"Beware them both… but most of all beware this boy" (Ignorance)
Anthropomorphism — abstract social problems become starved children. The real monsters aren't ghosts: they're ignorance of poverty and the want it creates. A society's children, not nature's.
Quote 4 — Ghost of Christmas Yet to Come
"Phantom slowly, gravely, silently, approached"
Three adverbs slow the sentence. The ghost doesn't speak — silence is more terrifying than words. Represents death and consequence. Forces Scrooge to face his own mortality without excuse.
💰 Poverty
Quote 1
"If they would rather die, they had better do it, and decrease the surplus population"
Scrooge's brutal attitude in Stave 1 — a real Victorian upper-class view. Dickens puts it in Scrooge's mouth so readers recognise and are appalled by attitudes they may have shared. The Ghost quotes it back to him in Stave 3.
Quote 2
"squeezing, wrenching, grasping, scraping, clutching, covetous old sinner"
Six verbs stacked together — the accumulation mirrors his accumulation of wealth. All taking, no giving. The physical violence of the verbs makes his greed feel aggressive and relentless.
Quote 3 — Cratchits
"His threadbare clothes darned up and brushed, to look seasonable"
Bob patches worn-out clothes just to look respectable for Christmas. Dignified poverty. Dickens presents the poor as hardworking and decent — the direct opposite of the 'lazy poor' myth.
Quote 4
"Bob had but fifteen 'Bob' a week himself"
Dark wordplay: the man's name equals his shillings. His identity is literally reduced to the minimum Scrooge pays him. Dickens uses wit to highlight economic exploitation.
✨ Redemption
Quote 1 — Marley
"You have yet a chance and hope of escaping my fate"
The entire novella's premise in one line. Redemption is possible but must be actively chosen. Marley represents the 'too late' outcome — he can warn Scrooge but cannot save himself.
Quote 2 — The turning point
"Scrooge hung his head to hear his own words quoted by the Spirit, and was overcome with penitence and grief"
The hinge moment. His own cruel words about the poor are thrown back when Tiny Tim's fate is discussed. He can no longer escape his own cruelty. Self-awareness begins redemption.
Quote 3 — Scrooge transformed
"I am as light as a feather, I am as happy as an angel, I am as merry as a schoolboy"
Three similes — joy floods back rapidly. Total contrast with the cold dense description in Stave 1. Dickens shows redemption as physical liberation: the whole body feels the difference.
Quote 4
"His own heart laughed"
Simple and devastating. The heart described as stone-cold at the start can now laugh. Internal change produces genuine joy. Dickens's argument: generosity makes you happier than greed ever could.

Higher mark points — say these in answers

Scrooge represents all selfish wealthy Victorians — this is social critique, not just a ghost story
Dickens argues poverty is caused by society, not by individual laziness or moral failure
Tiny Tim is a symbol — the most vulnerable child, saved only by the wealthy choosing generosity
Dickens chose a story over a pamphlet because emotion changes minds that facts can't reach
A Christmas Carol — Key Moments
Structure note: The chapters are called Staves, not chapters — a stave is a verse in a carol. The novella is literally structured as a Christmas song, moving from darkness to light. Know what happens in each stave and why it matters.
1
Stave 1 — Marley's Ghost
We meet Scrooge — cold, isolated, cruel. He turns away charity collectors, dismisses his nephew Fred's Christmas dinner invitation. That night Marley's ghost appears, dragging chains made of his sins, and warns Scrooge that three spirits will visit him. Links to: Supernatural (ghosts as moral agents) and Redemption (Marley represents the 'too late' outcome — Scrooge still has a chance).
"I wear the chain I forged in life" / "You have yet a chance and hope of escaping my fate"
2
Stave 2 — Ghost of Christmas Past
Scrooge is shown his lonely childhood at boarding school, then the warm Fezziwig Christmas party where he learned what generosity looks like, and finally Belle breaking off their engagement because he became obsessed with money. He sees exactly how and when he lost his humanity. Links to: Redemption (he must understand where he went wrong before he can change) and Poverty (contrast between Fezziwig's generosity and Scrooge's current meanness).
"You are changed. When it was made, you were another man."
3
Stave 3 — Ghost of Christmas Present
Scrooge visits the Cratchit family — poor, joyful, in love with life despite having almost nothing. He meets Tiny Tim, who the ghost warns will die if things don't change. The ghost then reveals two starved children beneath his robe: Ignorance and Want. The emotional turning point: the Ghost quotes Scrooge's own cruel words back at him. Links to: Poverty (the Cratchits as Dickens's argument against the 'lazy poor' myth) and Supernatural (Ignorance and Want as social monsters).
"Scrooge hung his head to hear his own words quoted by the Spirit"
4
Stave 4 — Ghost of Christmas Yet to Come
A silent, hooded phantom shows Scrooge a future where people steal his belongings when he dies, Tiny Tim's chair sits empty, and nobody mourns him — in fact people are relieved. The ghost takes him to a neglected grave covered in weeds. Scrooge sees his own name on the stone. Links to: Redemption (fear of this future drives his transformation) and Supernatural (death itself given human form).
"Phantom slowly, gravely, silently, approached"
5
Stave 5 — Scrooge Transformed
Scrooge wakes on Christmas morning, overjoyed. He buys the prize turkey for the Cratchits, reconnects with his nephew Fred, raises Bob Cratchit's wages, and becomes a second father to Tiny Tim. His redemption is concrete — he doesn't just feel better, he acts. Links to: Redemption (the full arc complete) and Poverty (Dickens's message: the wealthy can save lives if they choose to).
"I am as light as a feather, I am as happy as an angel" / "His own heart laughed"

The arc in one sentence per theme

Supernatural: Ghosts don't frighten — they educate. Each one shows Scrooge a truth he has been avoiding
Poverty: The Cratchits prove poverty ≠ laziness or misery. Scrooge proves wealth ≠ happiness
Redemption: Anyone can change — but they must choose to act, not just feel guilty
